Roy Clay Sr.

Godfather of Silicon Valley

Roy L. Clay is a computer science pioneer who was dubbed the “Godfather of Silicon Valley.” He is best known for developing new software for Hewlett-Packard (HP) computers. Over 50 years, Clay has played a huge role in computer science development and has paved the way for other African-Americans in the industry.

    Here's a timeline of Mr. Clay's professional life:
  • 1929: " Born in the small all-black town of Kinloch, Missouri."
  • 1951: " Graduated from St. Louis University, majoring in Mathematics"
  • 1956: " Found work as a programmer of IBM and Burroughs computers in the McDonnell Aircraft Corporation, which later became McDonnell Douglass, a leading aircraft manufacturer at the time."
  • 1958: " Worked as a computer programmer for what is now known as the Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory, a leading physics research lab operated by U.C. Berkeley for the U.S. Department of Energy, where he wrote software that demonstrated how radiation particles would spread after above-ground nuclear explosions."
  • 1962: " Worked as Fortran/COBOL manager for Control Data Corporation, the third largest mainframe computer manufacturer at the time (behind IBM and Sperry Rand), where he developed software languages for Control Data computers."
  • 1965: " Mr. Clay was the software development manager and lead developer for the HP2116A, the first computer sold by HP. Clay and his team were able to improve the reliability and size of computing. The HP2116A was only the second 16-bit computer on the market, after the Honeywell DDP-116. While at HP, Clay was the first Director of the HP Research and Development Software and Hardware Group, as well as a founding member of the HP Computer Division. Roy Clay also became the interim General Manager of the HP Computer Division following the departure of Tom Perkins, who went on to co-found the iconic venture capital firm Kleiner Perkins Caufield & Byers. In addition, as HP's most senior African-American at the time, Roy Clay also helped a number of other minorities launch successful careers in the tech industry, such as industry notable Ken Coleman."
  • 1971: " Worked as a consultant that specialized in identifiying and nurturing prospective investments in computer technology start-ups for Kleiner Perkins Caufield & Byers, the premiere venture capital firm at the time. Three of those investments - Tandem Computers ($3B), Compaq ($22B), and Intel ($110B) - eventually attained a combined valuation of $135 billion dollars. "
  • 1973: " Became the first non-Caucasian to serve on the Palo Alto City Council. He served as a councilman for Palo Alto, CA, from 1973 until 1979 and he served as the first African-American Vice Mayor of Palo Alto in 1976 and 1977. "
  • 1977: " Developed the automated Dielectric Withstand (Hipot) Test, which ensures that electrical products don’t shock or start fires. This testing technology, automated in the manufacturing process, was the first of its kind."
  • 1977: " Left HP as the highest-ranking African American to start his own company, ROD-L Electronics, the hipot and electrical-safety test equipment manufacturer where he continues to serve as CEO today. Manufacturers such as HP and GE use such testers to validate the electrical-safety and integrity of the line power devices that they produce, from computers to dishwashers."
  • 1988: " Became the first African-American member of the Olympic Club. "
  • 1999: " Founded the Virginia Clay/Unity Care Annual Gold Club Classic in honor of his late wife. "
  • 2003: " Clay was inducted into the Silicon Valley Engineering Council's Hall of Fame."
  • 2015: " Roy was conferred with the Silicon Icon Award"
